## Working Papers

+ **The Resource Blessing. How Commodity Booms Ended Territorial Disputes in South America**

    <a href="javascript:void(0)" onclick="toggleAbstract('abstract1')">Abstract</a>

  <div id="abstract1" style="display:none; margin-top:8px;">
  Economic development needs political order, as the state must be able to credibly commit to enforcing contracts. This capacity does not only hinge on institutions constraining rulers’ <em>willingness</em> to enforce rules, but also on their <em>ability</em> to do so amid external threats. Border agreements are costly signals designed for neighboring states to credibly commit to stable borders—securing property rights and state control—providing the certainties investors need in contested regions. This article traces the origins of stable borders in South America to the economic opportunities created during the “<em>first globalization</em>" (1870–1914). Capital-intensive commodity booms raised the opportunity cost of unresolved territorial disputes in resource-rich or highly suitable areas. As large investments were needed to exploit these opportunities, states sought to establish stable borders to reduce jurisdictional and policy uncertainties for potential investors. Empirically, the article examines how the Amazon Rubber Boom (1889-1912) accelerated the end of South American territorial disputes over territories highly suitable for the growth of para rubber. Survival analysis leveraging exogenous variation in land suitability for para rubber across disputed territories and in rubber prices over time shows that increases in rubber prices accelerated the resolution of disputes over rubber-suitable territories relative to other disputed territories. The study aims to contribute to our understanding of the interplay between economic and political development, and specifically to the literature on state formation, territorial claims, and the resource curse.
  </div>
